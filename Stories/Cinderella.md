## Summary

A kind girl, humiliated by her stepmother and stepsisters, is visited by her fairy godmother who transforms a pumpkin into a coach, mice into horses, and rags into a gown. She is sent to the ball with one condition: she must leave before midnight, or everything will revert. She goes, dances with the prince, and flees at midnight — leaving behind a glass slipper. The prince searches the kingdom for the foot that fits the slipper. It fits only her. Her sisters are astonished when she produces the matching slipper from her own pocket.

## Functional Pieces

### The Transformation Described Backwards

**Source text:**
> "if she stayed one moment longer, the coach would be a pumpkin again, her horses mice, her coachman a rat, her footmen lizards, and that her clothes would become just as they were before."

**Load-bearing words:** "again" — "just as they were before"

**Produces:** beauty shadowed by its own impermanence, joy as countdown

**The craft:**
The godmother describes the reversal in the same breath as the transformation. "Again" is the word that does the damage — the coach will be a pumpkin again, as if the pumpkin is the default and the coach is a brief deviation. The listener hears the beauty and simultaneously hears its expiration. Every moment at the ball is already the moment before midnight. The craft decision is to install the reversal inside the gift, so that the transformation and its undoing are announced together. What Cinderella receives is not simply beauty — it is beauty with a visible end date, which is a different thing. The slipper survives because she forgot it in her flight; everything else reverts. The story argues that the only things that last are the ones you leave behind by accident.

### The Slipper That Fits Like Wax

**Source text:**
> "He had Cinderella sit down, and, putting the slipper to her foot, he found that it went on very easily, fitting her as if it had been made of wax."

**Load-bearing words:** "fitting her as if it had been made of wax"

**Produces:** recognition, the sense that identity is a physical fact not a social performance

**The craft:**
"As if it had been made of wax" — the slipper does not merely fit; it conforms, like something that grew around her foot specifically. This is the craft decision that separates the recognized from the pretenders: the slipper argument bypasses beauty, wealth, and claim. It is a fact of physical reality. The stepsisters can dress like her, claim to be her, present themselves at the door — but the slipper will not lie. Identity, the story argues, is something the body knows. It cannot be performed into existence. This is both the fairy tale's comfort and its cruelty: the right person is recognized, but recognition requires evidence, and the evidence is not something you can manufacture.

### The Second Slipper from Her Pocket

**Source text:**
> "Her two sisters were greatly astonished, but then even more so, when Cinderella pulled out of her pocket the other slipper, and put it on her other foot."

**Load-bearing words:** "pulled out of her pocket" — "even more so"

**Produces:** revelation, the silencing of doubt

**The craft:**
She says nothing. She produces the slipper and puts it on. The structure of this moment is: the first slipper proves she is the girl from the ball; the second slipper proves she has been herself all along. The first is recognition. The second is disclosure — she was always carrying the evidence of who she was. "Pulled out of her pocket" is the craft precision: the slipper was there the entire time, in her possession, in a pocket. She knew. She waited. The stepsisters' astonishment is "even more so" because the second slipper is not just confirmation — it is evidence that she was never the passive, accidental girl they thought she was. She walked into the room already holding the proof. The argument is not made in words. It is made by putting on the shoe.

## Cross-references

- See [[Rumpelstiltskin]]: **Evidence Over Argument** — in Rumpelstiltskin, the queen wins by speaking a name; in Cinderella, the heroine wins by producing a slipper. Both stories resolve through a single piece of evidence that cannot be argued with. The person who holds the proof wins without having to explain themselves.
- See [[Sleeping-Beauty]]: **The Time Limit as Shadow** — Cinderella's midnight and Sleeping Beauty's hundred years are both temporal structures installed within the gift. In both stories, the gift is given with its own limit already embedded. The craft decision in both cases is to make the listener aware of the limit from the beginning, so that the time inside it is already shadowed.
