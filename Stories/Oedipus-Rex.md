## Summary

The oracle tells King Laius that his son will kill him and marry his wife. Laius has the infant Oedipus left on a hillside to die. The baby survives, is raised by the King of Corinth, and grows up. Oedipus hears the same prophecy about himself and flees Corinth to avoid it. On the road he kills a stranger — his real father, Laius — and arrives at Thebes, where he solves the Sphinx's riddle and is given the widowed queen Jocasta as his wife. Years later, investigating a plague, Oedipus unknowingly investigates himself. He learns the truth. He blinds himself. Jocasta hangs herself.

## Functional Pieces

### The Prophecy That Builds the Road It Predicts

**Source text:**
> "This is what prophets and prophecies are worth! Have no dread of them."
> *(Jocasta, dismissing the oracle — spoken while every step of the prophecy has already been fulfilled)*

**Load-bearing words:** "have no dread of them"

**Produces:** dramatic irony, dread through negation

**The craft:**
Jocasta says "have no dread" at the precise moment when dread is most warranted. This is Sophocles' central structural technique: the characters who deny the prophecy are the ones who fulfilled it. Laius tried to kill the infant to prevent the oracle — that act sent Oedipus to Corinth, where he heard the oracle about himself and fled, which brought him back to the road where Laius was travelling. The attempt to escape the prophecy was the mechanism of its fulfillment. Jocasta's dismissal of prophets — delivered with confidence, to reassure — is itself a further step in the same pattern. Every act of rational resistance to the oracle has served it. The craft decision is to let the audience know this while the character does not.

### The Investigation That Convicts the Investigator

**Source text:**
> "I, Oedipus, Oedipus, damned in his birth, in his marriage damned, Damned in the blood he shed with his own hand!"

**Load-bearing words:** "Oedipus, Oedipus" — "his own hand"

**Produces:** recognition, catharsis, the horror of self-knowledge

**The craft:**
The repetition of the name — "Oedipus, Oedipus" — is the recognition itself performed in language. He says his name twice as if testing whether it is still his, as if saying it once was not enough to confirm that this is him, that this happened to him, that he is the man he has been hunting. "His own hand" carries the full weight: he is the murderer, the investigation is over, and the hand that committed the crime is the same hand that held the scepter, the same hand now. The craft decision throughout the play is to make Oedipus the most vigorous investigator of his own guilt. He drives the inquiry, dismisses warnings, demands the full truth. His virtue — his determination to know — is what destroys him. The same construction as Orpheus: the act of love that kills. Here, the act of justice.

### The Self-Blinding

**Source text:**
> "O Light, may I look on you for the last time!"
> *(spoken in the moment before he blinds himself)*

**Load-bearing words:** "O Light" — "for the last time"

**Produces:** catharsis, the rightness of the punishment

**The craft:**
He has been, throughout the play, a man who could not see what was directly in front of him, while the blind prophet Tiresias saw everything. The self-blinding is not simply punishment — it is the completion of an irony that has been running since the beginning. He had eyes and did not see; now he will have no eyes and will finally know. The invocation of light — "O Light" — in the last moment before darkness is another precise reversal: he addresses the thing he is about to destroy. "For the last time" is the craft precision, because it signals that he knows exactly what he is about to do and is not flinching from it. The punishment is chosen, administered by his own hand, calibrated to fit the specific shape of the crime.
