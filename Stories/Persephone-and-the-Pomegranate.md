## Summary

Hades abducts Persephone and takes her to the underworld. Her mother Demeter searches the earth for her, and in her grief lets all crops fail. Zeus eventually orders Persephone's return — but Hades, before releasing her, secretly gives her pomegranate seeds to eat. Because she has tasted food in the underworld, she is bound to return for one third of every year. The seasons are the consequence.

## Functional Pieces

### The Secret Feeding

**Source text:**
> "he on his part secretly gave her sweet pomegranate seed to eat, taking care for himself that she might not remain continually with grave, dark-robed Demeter."

**Load-bearing words:** "secretly" — "taking care for himself"

**Produces:** dread, violation, the feeling that the game was already rigged

**The craft:**
"Secretly" is the load-bearing word of the entire myth. Hades does not force the seeds into her mouth — he gives them to her covertly, which means from the outside it looks like she ate of her own will. The cosmic law that follows does not distinguish between consent and its absence. "Taking care for himself" reveals the calculation: this is not hunger, not hospitality, not accident. It is deliberate insurance against a full loss. The craft decision is to make the act small — a few seeds, done quietly, without violence — and then make the consequence total and permanent. The disproportion between the act and its consequence is the source of the dread. Something almost nothing has bound the world.

---

### The Contradiction in Testimony

**Source text:**
> "he secretly put in my mouth sweet food, a pomegranate seed, and forced me to taste against my will."

**Load-bearing words:** "forced me to taste against my will"

**Produces:** injustice, helplessness, the inadequacy of truth as a defense

**The craft:**
Persephone says she was forced. The cosmic law does not care. This is the precise mechanism that makes the myth do its work: the distinction between choice and compulsion, which matters enormously in human law, is irrelevant to the law of the underworld. Her testimony is true and useless. The detail "sweet food" is also deliberate — the word is appealing, even seductive, which blurs the force she describes. Sweet food forced against her will. The contradiction holds both things at once, and the law resolves it by ignoring both.

---

### The Proportional Sentence

**Source text:**
> "if you have tasted food, you must go back again beneath the secret places of the earth, there to dwell a third part of the seasons every year: yet for the two parts you shall be with me and the other deathless gods."

**Load-bearing words:** "a third part" — "yet for the two parts"

**Produces:** inevitability, a sense that this was always how the world was going to be

**The craft:**
The sentence is mathematically precise: one seed, one third of the year. The precision makes the rule feel pre-existing — not invented as punishment, but revealed as a law that was always already there. "Yet for the two parts" is the other craft decision: the sentence is not total. She is not lost. The two thirds she keeps becomes a concession, and the framing of concession makes the one third sound survivable. What the myth produces through this construction is not tragedy but cosmological explanation — the seasons are not random, they are the exact measure of a theft.
