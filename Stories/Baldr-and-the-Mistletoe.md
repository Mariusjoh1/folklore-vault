## Summary

Baldr, the most beloved of the gods, begins having dreams of his own death. The gods, alarmed, have his mother Frigg extract oaths from every thing in existence — fire, water, metal, stone, animals, diseases — swearing not to harm him. One thing is skipped: the mistletoe, which Frigg judges too young and insignificant to bother with. Loki, disguised as a woman, questions Frigg and learns of the exception. He fashions a dart from the mistletoe and brings it to the blind god Höd, guiding his hand so the dart flies at Baldr during the gods' game of throwing things at him. The dart goes through him. Baldr falls dead. It is the beginning of the end of the world.

## Functional Pieces

### The Exception Created by Kindness

**Source text:**
> "A shoot of wood grows west of Valhalla. It is called mistletoe, and it seemed too young for me to demand its oath."
> *(Frigg to Loki, disguised as a woman — Prose Edda, Gylfaginning)*

**Load-bearing words:** "it seemed too young" — "to demand"

**Produces:** dread through irony, the horror of good judgment leading to catastrophe

**The craft:**
Frigg does not skip the mistletoe out of carelessness or arrogance. She skips it out of what sounds like mercy — it seemed too young to burden with an oath. "To demand" carries the weight: there is something in Frigg that finds it wrong to require a vow from something so small and new. This is the precise craft decision that makes the story function. The thing that kills Baldr is not overlooked because Frigg was negligent. It is overlooked because she was kind. The same mechanism as Orpheus and Oedipus: the virtue that motivates the action is the thing that creates the gap through which catastrophe enters.

### The Blind God's Innocent Hand

**Source text:**
> "Höd took the mistletoe and shot at Baldr at Loki's direction. The missile flew through him and he fell dead to the ground, and this was the unluckiest deed ever done among gods and men."
> *(Prose Edda, Gylfaginning)*

**Load-bearing words:** "at Loki's direction" — "the unluckiest deed"

**Produces:** grief, the diffusion of guilt, cosmic wrongness

**The craft:**
Höd is blind. He does not know what he is throwing or at whom he is throwing it. The actual killer is innocent. Loki is the mind of the murder; Höd is the hand; Baldr is struck by a throw that was, from Höd's perspective, just another game. This distributes the crime across three parties in a way that makes it impossible to locate. The narrator's judgment — "the unluckiest deed ever done among gods and men" — is the other craft decision. Not "the most evil" or "the most wicked." Unlucky. The word implies contingency, the sense that a different outcome was possible, that this was not fated but happened anyway. That contingency is what makes it feel like a wound rather than a verdict.

### The Gods' Game That Became the Death

**Source text (implied structure):**
The gods spent their time throwing weapons at Baldr for sport, knowing nothing could harm him. This was entertainment. The game was the context Loki used.

**Load-bearing words:** the game itself — the context of play

**Produces:** the horror of the ordinary turned deadly

**The craft:**
Before the mistletoe, the gods had been throwing axes, swords, and stones at Baldr as a game — because nothing could harm him, it was a display of his invulnerability. Loki does not create a new context for the murder. He uses the existing one. The killing happens inside the joy. The party does not stop for the murder to occur; the murder happens at the party. This is the structural decision that makes Baldr's death feel so wrong — there is no dramatic buildup, no confrontation, no fight. He dies in the middle of a game. The ordinariness of the context amplifies the loss.
