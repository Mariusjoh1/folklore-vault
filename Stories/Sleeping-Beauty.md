## Summary

At the christening of a princess, twelve fairies bestow gifts. A thirteenth fairy, uninvited and furious, curses the princess: she will prick her hand on a spindle and die. A twelfth fairy who has not yet given her gift softens the curse — the princess will not die but sleep for a hundred years, until a king's son comes to wake her. The curse is fulfilled. The whole castle sleeps with her. A hundred years later a prince cuts through the forest that has grown up around the castle, finds the princess, and wakes her. Her first words are: "Is it you, my Prince? You have waited a long while."

## Functional Pieces

### The Curse That Cannot Be Undone, Only Redirected

**Source text:**
> Old fairy: "the princess should prick her hand with a spindle and die of the wound."
> Young fairy: "The Princess shall indeed pierce her hand with a spindle; but, instead of dying, she shall only fall into a deep sleep, which shall last a hundred years."

**Load-bearing words:** "shall indeed" — "instead of dying" — "only"

**Produces:** dread with relief, but relief that knows its own limit

**The craft:**
"Shall indeed" is the load-bearing phrase. The young fairy does not say the curse is broken or wrong. She confirms it: the pricking will happen, the prophecy will fulfill itself. What she offers is not a cancellation but a substitution. "Instead of dying, she shall only fall into a deep sleep" — the word "only" is doing difficult work: it frames a hundred-year sleep as the lesser evil, which technically it is, but the word normalizes what is still a catastrophe. The craft decision is to establish that curses have momentum. Evil set in motion cannot be stopped; it can at best be shaped. This is a different claim about the world than most fairy tales make — it is not "good defeats evil" but "good can negotiate with evil's consequences."

### The Spindle That Acts

**Source text:**
> "no sooner had she seized the spindle than she pricked her hand and fell down in a swoon."

**Load-bearing words:** "no sooner" — "fell down in a swoon"

**Produces:** inevitability, the curse having its own agency

**The craft:**
"No sooner had she seized the spindle than" — the consequence is instantaneous, attached to the act with no gap. But the more precise craft decision is the spindle itself. The earlier passage says the spindle "ran into her hand." The object acts; the princess is the surface it acts on. She does not make a mistake. She does not prick herself carelessly. The thing fulfills the curse as if it has been waiting for this moment. This passivity is deliberate: the princess's individual choices are irrelevant to what is going to happen. The curse operates on a plane above volition. What produces the dread is the sense that there was nothing she could have done.

### "You Have Waited a Long While"

**Source text:**
> "the Princess awoke, and looking on him with eyes more tender than could have been expected at first sight, said:-- 'Is it you, my Prince? You have waited a long while.'"

**Load-bearing words:** "more tender than could have been expected" — "You have waited a long while"

**Produces:** wonder, intimacy, the sense that the sleep was not nothing

**The craft:**
These are the first words she speaks after a hundred years of sleep. Not "where am I?" Not "who are you?" She recognizes him and she acknowledges his wait. The narrator's aside — "more tender than could have been expected at first sight" — is the clue: Perrault is signaling that this is not a normal first meeting, that something happened during the sleep that prepared her for him. "You have waited a long while" implies awareness of the wait, which implies a kind of consciousness during the sleep. The craft decision is to leave this completely unexplained. She simply knew. The intimacy is presented as a fact without a mechanism, which makes it feel like something that belongs to a different register than ordinary logic — exactly the register of myth.

## Cross-references

- See [[Oedipus-Rex]]: **The Prophecy That Cannot Be Escaped** — both stories share the structure of a prophecy that fulfills itself despite attempts to prevent it. In Oedipus, the attempt to prevent the prophecy is the mechanism of its fulfillment. In Sleeping Beauty, the prophecy is partially softened but not prevented — the fulfillment still happens, only with modified terms.
- See [[Baldr-and-the-Mistletoe]]: **The Modified Sentence** — the young fairy's intervention mirrors the structure of Frigg's oath-taking. Both attempt to prevent a death. Both partially succeed and partially fail. The curse is not undone; it is redirected into a form that is bearable, but still a form of the original harm.
