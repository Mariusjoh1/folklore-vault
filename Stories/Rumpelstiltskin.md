## Summary

A miller, wanting to seem important, tells the king his daughter can spin straw into gold. The king locks her in a room full of straw and threatens her life if it is not gold by morning. A small man appears and spins it for her in exchange for her necklace, then her ring, then — when there is nothing left to give — her first child when she becomes queen. She becomes queen, has a child, and the small man returns to collect. She weeps; he offers her a way out: if she can guess his name within three days, she can keep the child. Her messenger, searching the forest, hears the man dancing around a fire and singing his name. She speaks it. He tears himself in two.

## Functional Pieces

### The Boast That Traps Someone Else

**Source text:**
> "I have a daughter who can spin straw into gold."

**Load-bearing words:** "I have a daughter"

**Produces:** injustice, the trap set by someone who faces no consequence

**The craft:**
The miller lies to seem important. He is the cause of everything that follows. But the story has no interest in him after this sentence — he disappears, faces no consequence, and is never mentioned again. His daughter is the one who will spend three nights in terror, give away a child, and spend three days trying to remember a name she has never heard. "I have a daughter" positions her as a possession and a boast simultaneously. She is the thing he is showing off. The craft decision is to build an entire story of injustice out of this one lie and then never return to its source. The originating cause is abandoned; only its consequences persist. This is the structure of a great deal of actual injustice.

### The Name Sung in the Dark

**Source text:**
> "Today do I bake, tomorrow I brew, / The day after that the queen's child comes in; / And oh! I am glad that nobody knew / That the name I am called is Rumpelstiltskin!"

**Load-bearing words:** "nobody knew" — "I am glad"

**Produces:** dramatic irony, the terror of being overheard

**The craft:**
He sings this alone in the forest, at a fire, convinced of his secrecy. "I am glad that nobody knew" is pride in the protection his name gives him — the name is his weapon, and he believes it is hidden. The irony is that the song is the mechanism of his undoing: the moment he sings it, he is overheard, and the protection dissolves. The craft decision is to make his undoing self-caused through the exact thing that marks his confidence. He celebrates his anonymity by singing his name. The structure is the same as Oedipus: the quality that makes him feel safe — his certainty that no one knows — is the quality that creates the disclosure. His joy is the evidence that he has already lost.

### The Self-Destruction

**Source text:**
> "he stamped his right foot into the ground so hard that his whole leg sank in. Then he grabbed his left leg with both hands and pulled so hard that he tore himself right in two."

**Load-bearing words:** "pulled so hard" — "tore himself right in two"

**Produces:** catharsis, the completeness of self-administered punishment

**The craft:**
He is not killed, imprisoned, or punished by anyone else. His own rage destroys him. This is the craft decision that separates Rumpelstiltskin from a story about justice and makes it a story about a particular kind of character. He would rather be destroyed than accept that he was beaten. "Pulled so hard that he tore himself right in two" — the violence is grotesque, total, and entirely self-directed. The queen did nothing except speak a name. His destruction is his own work. The story argues that this type of person — the one who cannot bear to lose — carries the instrument of his own ending inside him all along. The name was not his weakness. His need to win was.

## Cross-references

- See [[Oedipus-Rex]]: **The Self-Administered Punishment** — Oedipus blinds himself; Rumpelstiltskin tears himself apart. Both punishments are self-chosen, self-executed, and calibrated to the specific shape of the crime. Both refuse external punishment as insufficient.
- See [[Bluebeard]]: **The Boast as Engine** — in Bluebeard, the horror begins because the wife is given the keys and the prohibition. In Rumpelstiltskin, the horror begins because the miller tells a lie. Both stories build catastrophe from a character who makes a decision for reasons of pride or vanity and then faces no consequence, while someone else bears the entire cost.
