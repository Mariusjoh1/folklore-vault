# Running Patterns

This file is updated automatically each week by the folklore agent.
When the same craft technique appears across multiple stories, it is logged here with references.

---

## The Prohibition as Structural Prophecy

**First appearance:** Orpheus and Eurydice (Greek/Roman — Ovid, Metamorphoses Book X)

**Technique:** A condition or prohibition is placed on a character not as a moral test but as the announced architecture of the loss already coming. The prohibition is given in legalistic or impersonal language that strips it of the possibility of mercy. The reader understands at the moment the condition is stated that it will be broken — the story would not give us the rule if it were kept. The condition functions as dramatic irony built into the grammar of permission: it grants and withdraws simultaneously.

**What to watch for:** Prohibitions framed as contracts ("annulled," "void," "forfeit") rather than warnings. Conditions placed in subordinate clauses after a full grant. Any single-rule structure where the entire gift or prize hangs on one prohibition.

## The Love-Caused Destruction

**First appearance:** Orpheus and Eurydice (Greek/Roman — Ovid, Metamorphoses Book X)

**Technique:** The agent that destroys the thing the hero sought is the same quality that motivated the heroic action. Orpheus descends to the underworld because he loves Eurydice; he turns back because he loves Eurydice; turning back kills Eurydice. The cause of the journey and the cause of the failure are identical. This construction makes the story tragic rather than cautionary — it is impossible to say what he should have done differently without saying he should have loved her less, which the story refuses to permit as an answer.

**What to watch for:** Any story where the hero's destruction is caused by the specific virtue the story has already celebrated in them. The mechanism requires that the reader be unable to distinguish "what would have saved her" from "what would have made him someone else."

## The Non-Accusation as Grief Engine

**First appearance:** Orpheus and Eurydice (Greek/Roman — Ovid, Metamorphoses Book X)

**Technique:** The dying or departing character refuses to assign blame. By distributing fault or absorbing it entirely, they close off the reader's easiest emotional exit: resolution through condemnation. If the victim accuses, the reader can discharge grief by judging the guilty party. If the victim forgives or refuses to accuse, the grief has nowhere to land. Restraint in the final words — minimal, formal, quiet — amplifies this effect by creating a gap between the smallness of the word and the size of the loss.

**What to watch for:** Final speeches or last words where the expected recrimination is withheld. Single-word or single-clause farewells at the site of maximum loss. Any construction where the departing character takes on shared fault they did not earn.
