## Weekly Synthesis

This week's story reveals that the most durable grief structures are built not through accumulation but through precision of restraint. Ovid does not describe Eurydice's second death at length — he ends it in a single word, "Farewell," which is far too small for what it names, and the gap between the word and the loss is where all the feeling lives. The key craft insight from this analysis is the **cause-destruction identity**: the thing that motivates the heroic act and the thing that destroys the goal are the same — Orpheus looks back because he loves her, and looking back kills her — which makes the story impossible to resolve through judgment. The reader cannot decide what he should have done differently without deciding he should have been a different person, and the story will not permit that answer. The technique to carry forward: when you want to produce grief that does not close, make the cause of the failure identical to the virtue that made the action worth attempting. Condemnation becomes structurally impossible, and the emotion has nowhere to go except deeper.
